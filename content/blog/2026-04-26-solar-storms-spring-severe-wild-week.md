## When the Sun Gets Rowdy

This past week reminded us that weather isn't just about what's happening in our atmosphere — sometimes the action starts 93 million miles away. Our Sun kicked out an M-class solar flare that peaked at 2.27e-5 W/m2, which is significant enough to catch the attention of space weather forecasters. M-class flares sit right in the middle of the solar activity scale, strong enough to cause brief radio blackouts and potentially trigger aurora displays if Earth gets hit by the associated coronal mass ejection.

While we're still waiting on detailed Kp index readings to see how much geomagnetic activity actually reached us, this solar uptick is worth noting. The Sun is approaching solar maximum in its 11-year cycle, so we're seeing more frequent outbursts. If you're a ham radio operator or rely on GPS for precision work, keep an eye on space weather alerts this week.

## Shaking Things Up in Japan

Meanwhile, back on solid ground, Japan experienced a significant magnitude 7.4 earthquake about 100 kilometers ENE of Miyako this week. That's a substantial quake that likely triggered tsunami warnings across the Pacific Rim. While this doesn't directly impact U.S. weather patterns, major seismic events like this remind us that our planet is constantly active beneath the surface.

Closer to home, we saw a couple of notable tremors — including a magnitude 4.8 near Silver Springs, Nevada, and a magnitude 4.0 just west of Cooter, Missouri. The Missouri quake is particularly interesting since it's in the New Madrid seismic zone, an area that historically produces some surprising earthquake activity for the central United States.

Now for the weather pattern that's really starting to flex its muscles: tornado season across the Plains. We're hitting that sweet spot in late April where the jet stream starts getting more active, temperature contrasts sharpen between air masses, and the infamous "Tornado Alley" begins its spring awakening.

This week's setup looks particularly interesting. A strong upper-level trough is expected to dig into the central Plains midweek, creating exactly the kind of atmospheric tension that breeds severe thunderstorms. If you're anywhere from Oklahoma up through Kansas and Nebraska, Tuesday through Thursday could bring some serious weather. We're talking about the potential for supercells, large hail, damaging winds, and yes — tornadoes.

## What's Coming This Week

Looking ahead, the weather pattern is setting up for a classic late-April scenario. A powerful storm system will track from the Rockies into the Plains, dragging a cold front that means business. Ahead of this front, temperatures could soar into the 80s and even low 90s from Texas up through Kansas, while behind it, we're looking at a dramatic cooldown — potentially 30-40 degree temperature drops in some areas.

The Storm Prediction Center will likely be issuing enhanced risk areas for severe weather, particularly Tuesday and Wednesday. This isn't just garden-variety springtime storms — we're talking about the kind of setup that can produce long-track tornadoes and baseball-sized hail.

For those heading to outdoor events this week, timing will be everything. The Boston Marathon tomorrow should see decent conditions, but if you're planning any outdoor activities in the central Plains midweek, you'll want to stay glued to your weather radar and have multiple ways to receive warnings.
